Open Graphing Calculator

This is an open source graphing calcultor that I collaborated with a math professor that was inspired by Desmos. It was made using JavaScript, JSXGraph, jQuery, and MathQuill. There was a lot of things that we incorporated and a handful of things that we wanted to incorporate into the webapp, we completed the app within a month and moved on to other projects. At first we made sure that we can graph simple things such as x^2 and e^x and then we worked our way up from there. After we got the basics out of the way, we implemented points (both free moving points and points on a curve). At the same time we implemented calculus features into the graphing calculator, which you can view the Riemann's sum of the integral, get the derivative (to also get the tangent of the graph) and the second derivative. After creating the points I started to work on the geometry side of the app. By using the points the user can create lines, line segments, sectors of a circle, circles, parabolas, ellipses, hyperbolas, lagrange polynomials, and triangles. After which I implemented a custom function feature, so typing in MyFunction(x) = 2 x ^ 3 will create a new function that all other entry boxes can now use, so typing in a new entry box MyFunction(sin(x)) would produce a new graph that looks like this 2 (sin(x)) ^ 3. During the time Desmos has not implemented their version of custom functions. As of right now, Desmos' custom function feature is limited to only a single letter such as f(x) = x + 1 and g(a, b, t) = a + t (b - a), however it is more robust by implementing piecewise functions, setting the range of the variables going into the function, and multiple variables.

VPAT Impact Analysis Form

This project was a request from my boss who wanted to automate a frankly lengthy process. It's a process done in every University, but for context I will be talking about the University I attended (California State University, East Bay). To the layman, a VPAT is an acronym for Voluntary Product Accessibility Template. Which is just a very long form that gives the reader a descriptive view of software in context to accessibility. Since the process of reviewing a VPAT to make sure the product is accessible, we must access whether or not the product is of high enough impact to warrant a review. That pre-review process was a pen and paper process that requires the reviewer to look up things about the product, communicate with the requester, and spot any contradictions. Typically, on average a single review can take about 2 weeks. Because it's such a slow process, there is a large backlog of reviews. And because the reviewer has to find contradictions, some can slip through as accessible; which results in a law suit against the school.

We started by translating the paper form onto a web form and make it look the same. Then we gave it a points system, where lower impact options (such as Not Required for Academic Purposes) gives the user negative points to where higher impact options (such as Required for Academic Purposes) gives the user positive points. With this point system we gave a threshold of what would be a Low Impact, Low-Medium Impact, Medium Impact, Medium-High Impact, and High Impact. Which Low Impact is low enough of an impact that it is ok to let them purchase the product without any reprocussions and Low-Medium Impact is the same but with us explaining any discrepancies (e.g. The product is required for academic purposes, but it's a back-end product where no students will ever notice it's there).

Once we were done with the app and started using it for a while, we became much more efficient. A completed pre-VPAT review process went from taking us 2 weeks to within the same day it was submitted for review. And after some higher ups showed the app with other schools, we noticed that we have avoided loads of law suits that other universities has accidently gotten into. After which, we had the time to improve our efficiency, communication, workflow, and documentation on the process.
